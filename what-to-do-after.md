## The core truth to internalise

Reading is recognition; the exam tests **recall and application**. Right now you can probably *recognise* every concept when you see it. On 24 June you'll need to *produce* it from a blank screen, applied to a case you've never seen, with no notes. The gap between those two states is closed by **active retrieval and writing**, not by re-reading. Everything below is designed to close that gap.

# PART 1 — THE PLAN (what to do, in order)

## Step 1 — Active recall on every self-test (highest priority)
Each day-summary ends with a quick-fire self-test, and the full-course summary has a cheat sheet. Go through **every** question with the answers covered and **say or write the answer before revealing it.** Mark each as ✅ (got it cold), 🔶 (roughly), or ❌ (blank). Only ✅ counts as learned. Repeat the 🔶 and ❌ items the next day. This is the single highest-value activity — do it first and do it repeatedly.

## Step 2 — Sit the mock exam properly
**Closed-book, timed (~75–90 min), no summaries open.** Write full answers in prose, draw the decision tree by hand, then grade yourself against the key. Your score tells you where you stand:
- 35+ → strong; polish weak sub-parts.
- 28–34 → passing but thin; re-study the lowest-scoring topics and re-attempt those questions.
- Below 28 → not ready yet; revisit the relevant day summaries before re-testing.
Note *which* questions cost you points and map them back to the day (the mock's diagnosis section does this for you).

## Step 3 — Drill the decision-tree calculation until it's automatic
This is the one skill you cannot bluff. Do **three or four** trees from scratch — invent your own numbers, or reuse the SmartTriage/SmartMesh structure with different probabilities and costs. Each time: draw decision vs. probability nodes, probability-weight the chance branches (check they sum to 1), subtract the cost, compare branches, **state a recommendation.** Stop only when you can do one in a couple of minutes without hesitating.

## Step 4 — Master the recurring "difference between X and Y" pairs
The exam loves these. Make a single index card (physical or mental) and be able to give each distinction in one or two sentences cold:
- risk vs. ethical issue (+ a risk that is NOT ethical)
- risk vs. uncertainty
- measurable vs. quantifiable benefit
- mitigation vs. contingency
- business change vs. enabling change
- static NPV vs. real options
- ethics vs. morality
- project vs. programme vs. portfolio
- Habermas discourse ethics vs. ethical strategists

## Step 5 — Build your own one-page "brain dump" sheet
From memory (not by copying), write everything you'd want in the first 60 seconds of the exam onto one page: the risk formulas, the four benefit types, the BDN columns, the five cost categories, the five philosophical angles, Ryan & Gregory's four categories, the Winkler contrasts, the decision-tree node rules. The **act of reconstructing it from memory** is the study; the page itself is almost disposable. Redo it a day later and see what you dropped.

## Step 6 — Practise applying to a fresh case
The exam is scenario-based. Invent a quick IT scenario (e.g. "a city library installing AI self-checkout," "a hospital deploying a scheduling platform") and, for each major topic, write two or three sentences applying it to *that* case: what are the benefits/costs/risks, the ethical issues, the public-sector complications, the SDG mapping, the governance level. This trains the application muscle that generic recall doesn't.

## Step 7 — Final light pass (exam eve)
The day before: one calm read of the **full-course cheat sheet** and your own brain-dump page. Re-test only the items still marked 🔶/❌. **No new material, no cramming.** Sleep properly — recall performance collapses when tired, and this is a recall exam.

# PART 3 — THINGS ONLY YOU CAN DO (can't be outsourced to a summary)

These are the gaps a study document genuinely cannot fill. Take them seriously.

## 3.1 Verify the logistics yourself
I built these materials from the course files, but **you** must confirm the practical details directly from Canvas/the course team, because they're easy to get wrong and costly if you do:
- **Exact date, time, location, and login** for the Remindo exam (the manual says Wed 24 June 13:45 — confirm it hasn't moved).
- What you're allowed to bring (it's closed-book — confirm whether a basic calculator or scratch paper is permitted, which matters for the decision-tree question).
- Whether any **errata or "not exam material" flags** were announced in lectures (a few slides were explicitly marked *not* exam material).

## 3.2 Cross-check against the actual source material
My summaries are a study aid, **not** a replacement for the primary sources. Before relying on a point, especially anything you'll quote or a definition you're unsure of, **open the original** — the lecture slides, Ward & Daniel chapters, and the key papers (Nelson, Peixoto, Ryan & Gregory, Herath, Winkler, Lee & Shin). I may have emphasised or simplified things differently than your lecturer intends, and the lecturer sets the exam. Where my summary and a slide disagree, **the slide wins.**

## 3.3 Fill gaps from the microlectures and readings
The exam draws on **Ward & Daniel, the papers, lecture sheets, AND microlectures.** I worked from the materials in the project files; if there are microlectures or reading details not fully captured there, only you can watch/read them and slot them in. If any topic in the summaries feels thin to you, that's a signal to go back to its source.

## 3.4 Do the writing yourself
No one can build your retrieval strength for you. The self-tests, the mock, the brain-dump sheet, the decision-tree drills — these only work if *you* produce the answers from memory. Reading my model answers feels productive but mostly builds recognition. **The hand has to do the work the eyes can't.**

## 3.5 Judge your own readiness honestly
After the mock, be honest about your score and your blank spots rather than reassuring yourself. A 🔶 is not a ✅. If ethics or public sector (the 40%) still feel shaky, that's where the next hour goes — not into a topic you already know because it feels good to review.

## 3.6 Manage yourself, not just the material
Sleep, timing, and calm are part of a recall exam. You also know your own weak spots and anxieties better than any document — if decision trees make you nervous, do more of them; if you freeze on open questions, practise writing fast first drafts. That self-knowledge is yours alone to act on.
