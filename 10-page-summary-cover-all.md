# PART 1 — FOUNDATIONS (the spine that runs through every topic)

These foundations underpin almost every question, so they earn their place regardless of their own small weight.

## 1.1 What a business case is

A **business case** is a structured, evidence-based argument that an IT investment is worth making. It's used to **initiate, monitor, and evaluate** IT projects, and is **part of the IT governance toolbox** *(Lecture 01)*. A good one is **plausible, well-reasoned, well-evidenced, and well-analysed** (the four rigor criteria) *(Course manual)*. Its internal logic is **why → what → how**: *why* (drivers/problem) → *what* (objective and benefits) → *how* (the project and the changes that deliver them).

The full business-case document outline (group-assignment structure, but it frames exam thinking too) *(Course manual)*: executive summary; introduction; problem/opportunity & current situation (**focus on public sector**); business objective; analysis of options; **benefits**; **costs** (incl. **TCO**); **risks** & contingency; **ethical analysis & CSR** (philosophical angles + **UN SDG mapping**); **governance** (scope, stakeholders, GANTT); conclusion; appendices. Notice that sections 6–10 *are* the exam topics.

**As-Is vs. To-Be:** As-Is = how things work now (the baseline); To-Be = the target state after the project. The gap between them is, in effect, the benefit *(Lecture 01)*.

## 1.2 Why IT projects fail — Nelson (2008)

The single most-cited foundation. **Roughly two out of three IT projects are considered failures** (total failure, cost overruns, time overruns, or fewer features than promised) *(Nelson 2008, citing Standish)*. From 99 retrospectives across 74 organisations, the crucial finding: failures are overwhelmingly **process (45%) or people (43%)**, NOT technology (product 8%, technology 4%) — **none of the top 10 mistakes was a technology mistake.** The top three (occurring in ~half of projects): **poor estimation/scheduling, ineffective stakeholder management, insufficient risk management.** Half of the most infamous >$100m failures came from the **public sector** *(Nelson 2008)*.

**Exam angle:** "Why do IT projects fail?" → ~2 in 3 fail; causes are people/process not technology; top three = estimation/scheduling, stakeholder management, risk management. This connects to Costs (estimation), Risk (its own topic), and Public Sector (failure-prone).

# PART 2 — THE TWO HEAVY TOPICS (40% of the exam)

## 2.1 ETHICS (10% — joint heaviest)

**Ethics ≠ "the right decision."** Ethics is the *field* that asks what is good/right, recognises **multiple legitimate perspectives**, surfaces **values and dilemmas**, and accepts **there's no typical right answer** *(Effing, Ethics lecture)*. **Morality** = the norms/values actually held; **ethics** = the systematic *reflection* on those norms. The learning goal is to **apply philosophical angles and write a reflection on moral consequences** — so the marks come from *applying* lenses and showing they *conflict*, not from picking a winner.

**The five philosophical angles** *(Effing lecture)* — know each + be able to apply to an IT case:
- **Aristotle (virtue ethics):** right action flows from **good character** and **living up to full potential** (the good life). *Is building this the kind of thing a virtuous organisation does?*
- **Kant (categorical imperative / deontology):** act only on principles you could **universalise**; treat people as **ends, not merely means**. Duty over consequences. *Secret data harvesting fails both tests regardless of good outcomes.*
- **Habermas (discourse ethics / communicative action):** legitimacy via **pure, power-balanced, inclusive dialogue** undertaken **for its own sake**; **communicative reason** (an end in itself) over **instrumental reason** (goal-based). Engagement done purely as strategy is "amoral." All affected parties must participate *(Noland & Phillips 2010)*.
- **Levinas:** ethics begins in the **face-to-face encounter with the Other** and our **responsibility for the vulnerable.**
- **Machiavelli:** strategic realism — **results and power**; the ends can justify the means; engagement is instrumental.

**Two ethical camps for business (Noland & Phillips 2010):** **Habermas discourse ethics** (engagement is moral only as pure, non-instrumental dialogue) vs. **ethical strategists** (ethical stakeholder engagement *ought* to be part of strategy because business **is** the creation of value for **all** stakeholders — rejecting the **separation thesis**; Aristotelian/utilitarian).

**Smart cities — Ryan & Gregory (2019)** *(by Mark Ryan, UTwente Philosophy)*: smart city = ICT/SIS-driven city (Caragliu et al. 2009: social + ICT + life + economy + sustainable + participation). **Four ethical issue categories — MEMORIZE:**
1. **Conflicts of interest & bias** — *conflict of interest:* vendors like **IBM sell SIS to cities while also ranking those cities on their index** (self-serving); *bias:* emphasis on **technical fixes over political/social solutions** to urban problems (Kitchin 2015).
2. **Economic pressure** — cities ranked by "smartness," pressured to adopt before benefits proven.
3. **Inequalities** — job replacement by AI, digital divide, rural decline, power/wealth gaps.
4. **Privacy** — data control/ownership, tracking (WiFi-Enschede example), recognition, security-vs-privacy.
Measures to address: **anonymisation/aggregation, informed consent, algorithmic auditing, data-collection agreements, object-over-personal data**; the headline finding is that **bottom-up, collaborative, citizen/municipal-owned data** is the more ethical path.

**Risk vs. ethical issue (NEAR-CERTAIN):** a **risk** = uncertain event affecting **project outcomes** (PMBOK); an **ethical issue** = a **viewpoint-dependent moral impact on stakeholders/society**, about **norms and values**, viewable through a philosophical angle. **A risk that is NOT an ethical issue:** a budget overrun, unforeseen operational costs, or a temporary non-critical performance dip.

**Ethical impact analysis (the method):** identify implications (not necessarily a risk), weigh **opposing views**, **temporarily set aside your own opinions**, apply philosophical angles, and **map to the UN SDGs reflecting on positive AND negative external effects** *(Effing lecture)*.

## 2.2 PUBLIC SECTOR (10% — joint heaviest, exam-only)

The learning goal: understand the **characteristics and complexity** of public-sector IT decision-making. **Taxonomy** *(Sethibe et al. 2007)*: private = for-profit + non-profit (NPOs); public = government agencies + semi-government agencies.

**Private vs. public — Winkler (2013) — THE centrepiece table.** Public-sector IT is harder across ten attributes:

| Attribute | Private | Public |
|---|---|---|
| Goals | Shareholder value | **Multifaceted public value** |
| Stakeholders | Few | **Many / conflicting** |
| Environment | Less regulated | **Legal/formal constraints** |
| Incentives | High/market | **Low / "soft budgets" / scrutiny** |
| Risks | Lower aversion | **High aversion** |
| Competition | High | **Low / intergovernmental cooperation** |
| IT innovation | Competitive advantage | **Treated as necessity** |
| IT competencies | Varying | **Generally lower** |
| IT sourcing | Flexible contracting | **Complex tendering** |
| IT resources | Proprietary | **Shared** |

Why this makes the business case harder: you can't justify on **NPV alone** — you must argue **multifaceted public value** across many **conflicting stakeholders**, under **legal/procurement constraints**, **soft budgets**, **scrutiny**, and **high risk-aversion**. Real-world illustrations *(Ryan & Gregory 2019)*: **Helsinki protects staff jobs regardless of SIS efficiency** (chat-bots reallocate, not replace); **Copenhagen** kept a project running for citizen value beyond what a private partner would.

**Exam angle:** Cite **Winkler (2013)**; give ~5–6 attributes with the public-side contrast and explain *why* two of them add complexity. Use a concrete public example to lift the answer.

# PART 3 — THE CORE "LIGHTER" TOPICS (5% each)

## 3.1 BENEFITS (5%)

**Benefits management (Ward & Daniel):** *"the process of organising and managing such that the potential benefits arising from the use of IS/IT are actually realised."* The core truth: **technology alone yields nothing — benefits come only through organisational change.** Hence the shift **from technology delivery → benefits delivery** (value not cost; change-management not just IT-implementation; stakeholders involved; benefits review).

**Five-step process:** Identify & structure → Plan realisation → Execute → Review & evaluate → Exploit further (iterative).

**Drivers → objectives → benefits:** **drivers** = internal/external pressures (the why); **investment objectives** = organisational outcomes addressing the drivers, must be **SMART**; **benefits** = advantages to specific, owning stakeholders.

**Four benefit types (CLASSIC EXAM ITEM):** **Observable** (expert judgement) → **Measurable** (can measure, can't forecast improvement) → **Quantifiable** (improvement *can* be forecast) → **Financial** (money value). *The measurable-vs-quantifiable distinction is the trap.*

**Benefits Dependency Network (BDN):** **IS/IT enablers → enabling changes (one-off) → business changes (ongoing) → benefits → objectives** (drivers behind). **Business change** = new ongoing way of working; **enabling change** = one-off prerequisite. The BDN forces every benefit to trace back through the changes needed to deliver it.

## 3.2 COSTS (5%)

**Tangible** costs = measurable; **intangible** costs are real but hard to measure (loss of goodwill/customer satisfaction) — and yes, they exist in IT projects *(Harmelink L03)*.

**Five cost categories (Ward & Daniel):** purchase; internal development (FTEs); infrastructure; **cost of business changes (soft costs, e.g. training)**; ongoing. **TCO (Total Cost of Ownership)** = full whole-life cost (hardware, software, implementation, training, support/maintenance, architecture, data) — not just purchase price.

**Benefits–costs link:** benefits are realised through activities → activities need resources → resources are costs. So costs attach to the same BDN chain as benefits.

**Cost estimation:** **Jørgensen (2004) found no clear winner** between **expert judgement** (good for novel domains; but biased/misled) and **model-based/parametric** estimation (**COCOMO**, Boehm; treats software as predictable, breaks on scope change). Size measures: **lines of code** vs. **Function Point Analysis** (outputs, inquiries, inputs, internal files, external interfaces). Best practice: historical data, explicit assumptions, averages, iterative range-based estimates.

## 3.3 RISKS (5%)

**Risk (PMBOK):** an uncertain event/condition that, if it occurs, has a **positive or negative** effect on objectives (risks can be opportunities). **Risk vs. uncertainty (Peixoto et al. 2014):** risk = **known probability** ("known unknowns," analysable); uncertainty = **unknown probability** ("unknown unknowns," not analysable).

**The formulas (HIGH PRIORITY) (Effing):** **Mitigation** reduces **probability**; **Contingency** reduces **impact**; **Reduction = Mitigation × Contingency**; **Exposure = Risk − Reduction**.

**Process:** *Assessment* (identify → analyse → prioritise) + *Control* (plan → resolve → monitor). PMBOK's six: plan, identify, qualitative analysis, quantitative analysis, plan responses, control. **Qualitative** = PxI matrix (red/yellow/green); **quantitative** = EMV/decision trees for high-priority risks.

**Four response strategies:** **Avoid, Mitigate, Transfer, Accept** (opportunity twins Explore/Enhance/Share) — **even "accept" needs a documented plan**. The **risk register** is the main, continuously-updated output; the **risk breakdown structure** classifies by source (external, commercial, project-management, technical).

## 3.4 ECO / SUSTAINABILITY (5%)

**UN SDGs:** **17** goals, **set 2015**, **target 2030** (poverty, education, health, gender equality, clean energy, reduced inequalities, sustainable cities, climate action…). **Goal 11 (Sustainable Cities)** is central to smart cities — urban population grows by **1.5bn by 2030**.

**Public responsibility & CSR:** the **principle of public responsibility** (Preston & Post 1975) — business and society interpenetrate, so decisions must weigh public/environmental consequences. **CSR** optimises the **triple bottom line: economic, social, environmental** *(Herath et al. 2019)*.

**Herath et al. (2019) green computing:** sustainability/green IT can be justified in the business case as a **profit-driven CSR investment** (a **win-win**, not pure cost), best valued as a **real option** (NPV + the value of flexibility/waiting under carbon-tax uncertainty, via Bayesian updating) rather than plain NPV.

**Method (carried from ethics):** **map the project to specific relevant SDGs**, reflecting on **positive AND negative** external effects.

## 3.5 GOVERNANCE (5%)

**IT governance** = the high-level management/systems ensuring IT investments **contribute to the business and don't waste resources** *(Ward & Daniel 2012; Cambridge Dictionary 2022)*.

**Project vs. programme vs. portfolio** *(Harmelink L07)*:
- **Project** = single deliverable; narrow **time/cost/quality** success; finite (months–1yr+).
- **Programme** = interrelated projects implementing a **strategic initiative**; **benefits-based** success; multi-year; run via a **PMO**.
- **Portfolio** = the **entirety** of IT investments; **select & prioritise** for best benefit; enduring; run by a **CFO-type** controller; **aggregated** performance.

**Programme goals (Lycett et al. 2004):** *efficiency/effectiveness* (coordination, dependency management, resource use, knowledge transfer, senior-management visibility) + *business focus* (coherent communication, project definition, **alignment with drivers/strategy**). **Programme delivery issues:** keeping motivation alive, staying on track, reallocating/prioritising resources, inter-project communication.

## 3.6 TOOLS (5% — but the ONLY calculation skill)

**Real Options Theory.** Traditional **NPV/IRR** = **static** single number that **ignores flexibility** and undervalues uncertain projects *(Mun 2002)*. **Real options value = static NPV + option premium** (premium = value of flexibility). Definition *(Amram et al. 1999)*: **narrowly**, extending financial option-pricing to **real (non-financial) assets**; **broadly**, a way of thinking about **strategic options/future opportunities created by today's investments**. For **long-term strategic**, not operational, decisions.

**Option types (Mun 2012):** defer/wait, expand, abandon, contract, growth, choose, switch, phased. Like financial **call** (right to buy) / **put** (right to sell) options — a **right, not an obligation**.

**Decision trees (the calculation):**
- **Probability/chance node** → value = **expected value** (probability-weighted; probabilities sum to 1).
- **Decision node** → value = **maximum** of the available options.
- **Backward induction:** start at the end of the horizon, compute each preceding node back to the start.
- **Option value = value-with-option − value-without-option** (lecture's abandon example: 100 − 50 = 50).

*Worked method:* draw the tree → probability-weight the chance branches → subtract costs → compare branches → **recommend** explicitly. (Practice this by hand — it's the one skill you can't bluff.)

**PRINCE2 business case:** balances **Benefits against Cost and Risk**; key principle = **continued business justification** across the project's life (stop if justification is lost).

## 3.7 BCD — integration (5%)

The integrative goal: combine all the above into one **coherent, comprehensive** business case. There's rarely a standalone "BCD" question — instead it shows up as **case-based questions that require you to apply several topics to one scenario** (as the example exam does with "PublicCo / SmartCity 5.0"). The skill: tie every element back to the **specific organisation's** problem, drivers, and objective, and keep the **why → what → how** thread consistent.

# PART 4 — HIGH-YIELD CHEAT SHEET (the things most likely to earn points)

- **Failure stats (Nelson):** 2 in 3 fail; people/process not tech; top 3 = estimation, stakeholders, risk.
- **Benefit types:** observable → measurable → quantifiable → financial (measurable = can't forecast improvement; quantifiable = can).
- **BDN:** IS/IT enablers → enabling (one-off) → business (ongoing) changes → benefits → objectives.
- **Cost categories:** purchase, internal dev, infrastructure, business-change/soft, ongoing. **TCO** = whole-life cost.
- **Estimation:** Jørgensen (2004) — no clear winner expert vs. model; COCOMO = parametric.
- **Risk formulas:** mitigation↓probability, contingency↓impact; Reduction = M × C; Exposure = Risk − Reduction.
- **Risk responses:** Avoid, Mitigate, Transfer, Accept (accept still needs a plan).
- **Risk vs. uncertainty:** known vs. unknown probability (known vs. unknown unknowns).
- **Risk vs. ethical issue:** project-outcome event vs. viewpoint-dependent moral/societal impact (+ budget overrun as a non-ethical risk).
- **5 philosophical angles:** Aristotle (virtue), Kant (duty/universalise/ends-not-means), Habermas (pure dialogue/communicative reason), Levinas (the Other), Machiavelli (ends-justify-means).
- **Two business-ethics camps:** Habermas discourse ethics vs. ethical strategists (Noland & Phillips 2010).
- **Ryan & Gregory's 4 categories:** conflicts of interest & bias; economic pressure; inequalities; privacy. (IBM sells-and-ranks = conflict; technical-fixes-over-social = bias.)
- **Public sector (Winkler):** multifaceted public value; many conflicting stakeholders; legal constraints; soft budgets/scrutiny; high risk-aversion; complex tendering; shared resources.
- **SDGs:** 17, set 2015, target 2030; Goal 11 = sustainable cities. **Triple bottom line** = economic/social/environmental.
- **Green computing (Herath):** profit-driven CSR as a real option; win-win.
- **Real options:** value = static NPV + option premium; narrow/broad definition (Amram et al. 1999); decision-tree nodes (expected value vs. max) + backward induction.
- **PRINCE2:** Benefits vs. Cost & Risk; continued business justification.
- **Governance:** project (narrow/finite) → programme (strategic/benefits/PMO) → portfolio (all investments/prioritise/CFO). **Strategic Grid** (Ward & Peppard 2002): strategic / high-potential / key-operational / support.

# PART 5 — REVISION STRATEGY (where to put your hours)

1. **First, the 40%:** make **Ethics** and **Public Sector** airtight — the five philosophical angles applied to a case, the two ethics camps, Ryan & Gregory's four categories, and Winkler's public/private table. These are joint-heaviest and Public Sector is exam-only.
2. **Then, the un-bluffable 5%:** drill the **decision-tree calculation** by hand until it's automatic — draw, probability-weight, subtract cost, recommend.
3. **Then the recurring distinctions** the exam loves (all "difference between X and Y"): risk vs. ethical issue; risk vs. uncertainty; measurable vs. quantifiable benefit; mitigation vs. contingency; business vs. enabling change; static NPV vs. real options; ethics vs. morality; project vs. programme vs. portfolio.
4. **Then shore up** the remaining 5% topics (benefits, costs, risks, sustainability, governance) using the cheat sheet.
5. **Ignore Presentation** for the written exam (0% exam weight).
6. **Apply to the case:** the exam is scenario-based — always tie answers to the specific organisation. Generic textbook answers score lower than the same content applied.

**Pass = 28/50. Aim for 35+.** If you can do that on a closed-book mock, you're ready for **24 June**.
