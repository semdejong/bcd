# PART 1 — WHAT IS ETHICS? (THE FOUNDATIONS)

## 1.1 Ethics is not "the ethically right decision"

The lecture's opening move is deliberately disorienting *(Effing, Ethics lecture)*: **"ethics is something else than an ethically right decision."** The point is that ethics is **not** a procedure for spitting out the single correct answer. It is the *field of study* that asks the deeper questions:

- **What is good?**
- **What is right?**
- What do **different perspectives** and **multiple views** say?
- What are the **ethical values** at stake, and the **ethical dilemmas**?

And crucially: **there is no typical right answer.** An ethical dilemma is precisely a situation where reasonable people, applying different but legitimate moral frameworks, reach different conclusions. The famous illustration the lecture uses is the **trolley-problem-style AI dilemma** (from Moralmachine.net): should a self-driving system swerve to kill one person instead of several? Different ethical frameworks give different answers, and that's the whole point — ethics is about *reasoning through* competing values, not finding a formula.

**Exam angle:** If asked what ethics is, do **not** say "deciding what's right." Say ethics is the field that examines what is good/right, recognizes multiple legitimate perspectives, surfaces values and dilemmas, and accepts that there's often no single correct answer. This framing — *no typical right answer, multiple views* — is exactly what lets you score on a "reflection" question, because the examiner wants you to show you can hold opposing views rather than just assert one.

## 1.2 Ethics vs. morality

The lecture explicitly asks: **what is the difference between ethics and morality?** *(Effing, Ethics lecture)* The standard distinction (which the lecture builds on) is:

- **Morality** = the actual norms, values, and beliefs about right and wrong that a person or group *holds and lives by*. It's the first-order content — "this is wrong," "that is good."
- **Ethics** = the second-order, systematic *study and reflection* on morality — the philosophical examination of *why* something is right or wrong, and which frameworks justify which judgments.

In short: morality is the set of moral beliefs; ethics is the disciplined reasoning *about* those beliefs. This matters for the business case because you're asked to do **ethics** (reasoned reflection using philosophical angles), not merely state your **morality** (your personal opinion).

**Exam angle:** Be ready to distinguish them in one line — *morality = the norms/values held; ethics = the systematic reflection on those norms/values.* This is the kind of "difference between X and Y" question this exam loves.

# PART 2 — THE PHILOSOPHICAL ANGLES

The core skill for the exam is **applying various philosophical angles** to an IT innovation *(Course manual learning goal; Effing, Ethics lecture)*. The lecture names five thinkers as the toolkit: **Aristotle, Kant, Habermas, Levinas, and Machiavelli.** You need to (a) know what each represents and (b) be able to apply each to an IT case. Below, each is explained with its core idea and an IT-flavoured example. *(Note: the lecture slides name these philosophers with brief tags; the fuller explanations here give you the standard content behind each tag so you can apply them — examinably — to an IT scenario.)*

## 2.1 Aristotle — virtue ethics

**Core idea:** the right action flows from **good character** and the pursuit of living up to one's **full potential** (the "good life," *eudaimonia*). Aristotle's ethics is about *what kind of person/organization you should be* and cultivating virtues (honesty, courage, justice, practical wisdom) rather than following a rulebook or just counting outcomes. The lecture explicitly ties Aristotle to the idea of **"living up to full potential"** *(Effing, Ethics lecture, in the ethical-strategists section)*.

**Applied to IT:** ask not just "is this legal/profitable?" but "does building this system reflect good character and help people flourish?" *Example:* a company designing an AI hiring tool would ask whether the tool embodies fairness and integrity as virtues — whether deploying it is the kind of thing a *virtuous* organization does — rather than only whether it's efficient.

## 2.2 Kant — the categorical imperative (deontology)

**Core idea:** morality is about **duty and universal rules**, not consequences. Kant's **categorical imperative** says you should act only according to principles you could will to become a **universal law** — and you must treat people as **ends in themselves, never merely as means.** An action is right because of the *principle* behind it, regardless of how it turns out. This is **deontological** ethics (duty-based) *(Effing, Ethics lecture, "Kant — categorical imperative")*.

**Applied to IT:** ask "would it be acceptable if *everyone* did this?" and "does this system treat people as ends, or merely use them as data-points/means?" *Example:* harvesting users' personal data without consent fails the Kantian test twice — you couldn't universalize "secretly take everyone's data," and it treats users merely as a means to profit rather than as autonomous ends. Even if the data improves the product (a good *outcome*), Kant says the *principle* is wrong.

**Exam angle:** Kant is the cleanest contrast to consequentialist/utilitarian thinking — *rule and duty vs. outcome.* Being able to set Kant (right action = right principle, treat people as ends) against a consequences-based view is a strong move in a reflection answer.

## 2.3 Habermas — communicative action / discourse ethics

**Core idea:** moral legitimacy comes from **fair, undistorted dialogue** among all affected parties. Habermas distinguishes **instrumental reason** (goal-based — communicating in order to *get* something) from **communicative reason** (communication as an **end in itself**, aimed at mutual understanding) — and the Habermasian *prefers communicative reason* *(Effing, Ethics lecture, citing Noland & Phillips 2010)*.

The lecture, drawing on **Noland & Phillips (2010)**, lays out the discourse-ethics position in detail:

- **Stakeholder engagement done purely as strategy is, "by definition, amoral."** Morality of engagement is a **function of proper procedures** that keep the communication **"pure"** — free of power imbalances and undertaken for its own sake, not to serve a hidden purpose that would corrupt it.
- The goal is to **understand each other's views** — there is **no one universal truth**; instead, **intersubjective understanding** across various interests, and a **postmodern (subjective) truth concept**. Watch out for **company bias.**
- **Validity condition:** for outcomes of stakeholder engagement to be **morally valid**, the engagement must follow **strictly observed guidelines** — note that *"true answers can be acquired invalidly,"* i.e., getting a good result the wrong way doesn't make it morally valid. **All parties who will be part of, or affected by, the future interaction must participate.**
- It's **democratic in nature**: avoid imbalance or inequality in voice (the practical question the lecture poses: *"how to foster equality in voice?"*).
- **Problems:** it's **costly**, can provoke **organization of resistance**, can mean **power loss** for the firm, and is arguably **(too) idealistic.**

**Applied to IT:** before deploying a smart-city sensor network, a Habermasian would insist on genuine, power-balanced dialogue with *all* affected citizens — not a token consultation staged to legitimize a decision already made. The process must be fair for the outcome to be moral.

## 2.4 Levinas — ethics of the Other

**Core idea:** ethics begins in the **face-to-face encounter with the Other** and our infinite **responsibility for the other person**. For Levinas, the Other's vulnerability places an ethical demand on us that comes *before* any rule or calculation — responsibility is the foundation of ethics *(Effing, Ethics lecture, "Levinas")*.

**Applied to IT:** centre the concrete human being affected by the system, especially the vulnerable. *Example:* when designing an automated benefits-eligibility system, a Levinasian lens asks whether the system keeps the real, vulnerable claimant — the "face" of the Other — at the centre, or whether it reduces them to a case number and erases the ethical demand of their situation.

## 2.5 Machiavelli — strategic realism

**Core idea:** the pragmatic, power-and-results-oriented counterpoint. Associated with the view that securing the desired end can justify the means, and that effectiveness, self-interest, and the realities of power drive action *(Effing, Ethics lecture, "Machiavelli")*. In the ethics toolkit, Machiavelli functions as the hard-nosed, strategic, arguably *amoral/instrumental* pole — the opposite of Habermas's communicative ideal.

**Applied to IT:** the Machiavellian view would justify a data-grab or an aggressive rollout if it secures market dominance and the ends are achieved — engagement is instrumental, a means to an end. Including this angle in a reflection is useful precisely because it represents the position many real businesses actually take, which you can then critique against Kant, Habermas, or Aristotle.

**Exam angle (for all five):** The exam asks you to **apply** these angles, so practice running a single IT scenario (e.g. a facial-recognition system, an AI hiring tool, a smart-city data platform) through several lenses and showing how they *conflict*: Kant condemns the privacy violation on principle; a utilitarian/Machiavellian view might accept it for the aggregate benefit/competitive gain; Habermas asks whether affected citizens were genuinely consulted; Aristotle asks whether it reflects organizational virtue; Levinas asks whether the vulnerable individual is kept at the centre. **The marks come from showing the tension between angles, not from picking one "winner."**

# PART 3 — SMART CITIES AND THE RYAN & GREGORY (2019) PAPER

This is the most exam-likely *content* in the ethics block, because one of the two example questions is built directly on it. Ryan & Gregory (2019), "Ethics of Using Smart City AI and Big Data: The Case of Four Large European Cities," is the key paper — note it's by **Mark Ryan of the University of Twente's Department of Philosophy** *(Ryan & Gregory, 2019)*.

## 3.1 What is a smart city?

The definition is **contested** *(Ryan & Gregory, 2019; Effing lecture)*. Key points and definitions:

- **IBM coined the term in 2008**, referring to integrating "smart" technology into a city. The lecture gives two early definitions: **IBM (Harrison, 2010)** — an *"instrumented, interconnected and intelligent city"*; and **Hall et al. (2000)** — *"a city that monitors and integrates conditions of all of its critical infrastructures."* These frame **Smart City = ICT** *(Effing lecture)*.
- The **often-cited richer definition** is **Caragliu, Del Bo & Nijkamp (2009)**: a city is smart when investments in **human and social capital** and **traditional (transport) and modern (ICT) infrastructure** fuel **sustainable economic growth** and a **high quality of life**, with **wise management of natural resources, through participatory governance.** The lecture summarizes this as **Smart City = social + ICT + life + economy + sustainable + participation** *(Effing lecture)*.
- The common thread: heavy reliance on **SIS — Smart Information Systems** (i.e., **Big Data and AI**), plus **IoT** and **ICT** infrastructure, to manage the city *(Ryan & Gregory, 2019)*.

**Why it matters now:** the UN projects the urban population will grow by **1.5 billion by 2030** (to ~5 billion), straining resources, infrastructure, jobs, and healthcare — and smart cities are proposed as a creative response, tied to **UN Sustainable Development Goal 11 (sustainable cities and communities)** *(Ryan & Gregory, 2019, citing UN 2018)*. Because smart cities are still in their **infancy** and use diverse SIS, **many ethical challenges are still being defined.**

The paper's **research question:** *Which ethical issues arise in the use of SIS when striving towards smarter cities, and how can these ethical issues be addressed?* It answers this through a literature review plus interviews with **four European organizations**: **Amsterdam** (AI to understand citizens' complaints), **Helsinki** (a parking-permit chat-bot), **Copenhagen** (a data-exchange platform), and **Hamburg** (an open-source-algorithm project) *(Ryan & Gregory, 2019)*.

## 3.2 The four ethical issue categories — KNOW THESE

Ryan & Gregory group the ethical issues into **four categories** *(Ryan & Gregory, 2019; Effing lecture)*. This list is high-value; memorize all four with their content.

### (1) Conflicts of interest and bias — HIGHEST PRIORITY (in the example question)

- **Bias:** *"Smart city initiatives may place a greater emphasis on technical fixes, instead of implementing political and social solutions to try to tackle urban issues"* (Kitchin, 2015). In other words, the **bias is the over-reliance on technology as the fix**, neglecting the political and social roots of urban problems — an over-emphasis on "the smart" to the detriment of "the city."
- **Conflict of interest:** *"Companies are proposing ambitious plans for cities adopting their SIS technology, but it is important to identify the challenges and issues that may arise … to ensure the applications are ethically sound"* — naming big vendors like **Cisco, IBM, Huawei, Microsoft.** The sharpest example: a **conflict of interest since companies like IBM are currently selling SIS to cities while also ranking competing cities based on their index** — the same firm both sells the technology and judges/ranks the cities, which is self-serving.
- Underlying this is a **top-down vs. bottom-up** problem: responsible innovation favours including all stakeholders (bottom-up), but smart-city initiatives are often **top-down**, devised by tech corporations and city governments while **disregarding civic participation**, potentially prioritizing corporate/state interests over citizens *(Ryan & Gregory, 2019)*.

### (2) Economic pressure

Cities are **far from reaching the benefits promised** in smart-city agendas because they're still early-stage, yet there's pressure to **strive forwards** for **competitiveness** and **marketing effect** *(Ryan & Gregory, 2019; Effing lecture)*. Cities get **ranked by "smartness"** and that ranking affects national investment, foreign direct investment, and tourism — so there's economic pressure to adopt SIS to avoid losing out, even before the benefits are proven.

### (3) Inequalities

- **Human replacement by AI** (e.g. automated cleaning, automated surveillance) — fear of SIS replacing customer-service, driving, and factory jobs.
- A **geographic divide**: the most educated and prosperous citizens cluster in (smart) cities, with a **dramatic negative effect on the education, prosperity, and growth of rural areas.**
- The **digital divide**, **power asymmetries**, and the **wealth gap** *(Ryan & Gregory, 2019; Effing lecture)*.

### (4) Privacy

The richest category of examples *(Effing lecture; Ryan & Gregory, 2019)*:

- Protection of **property, physical space, and overall privacy.**
- **Control and ownership of data** (safeguarding data — who owns it?).
- **Movement tracking** (the lecture's local example: the **WiFi tracking case in Enschede**).
- **Recording of audio and video recognition.**
- Detecting **patterns in citizen behaviour** (shopping, parking, commuting).
- **AI body scanners.**
- The overarching tension: **security versus privacy.**

**Exam angle:** Be able to **name all four categories** (conflicts of interest & bias; economic pressure; inequalities; privacy) and explain each with an example. For the near-certain **conflicts-of-interest-and-bias** question, the model answer is: *conflict of interest* = vendors like IBM both selling SIS to cities and ranking those cities on their index (self-serving); *bias* = emphasizing technical fixes over political/social solutions to urban problems (Kitchin, 2015). The norm-answer file shows exactly this, so commit it to memory.

## 3.3 How to address the issues — measures and findings

Ryan & Gregory don't just diagnose; they offer **measures** to make SIS use more ethical *(Effing lecture; Ryan & Gregory, 2019)*:

- **Anonymizing and aggregation** of data.
- **Informed consent** (e.g. telling people *"you are now talking to a chat-bot"*).
- **Algorithmic auditing.**
- **Agreements for data collection.**
- Using **object data instead of personal data** where possible.

The paper's **practical findings** from the four cities: smart cities are genuinely in their **infancy**, so data availability/accuracy is a real limitation; **consent, transparency, and data ownership** were prominent concerns in every case, with a consensus that **data should belong to the citizen or municipality, not private companies**; and **collaboration (a public-private model) is at the heart of a successful smart city**, with a **bottom-up approach** seen as the most effective way to ensure a smart city actually works for and is used by its citizens *(Ryan & Gregory, 2019)*.

**Exam angle:** If asked how ethical issues can be *addressed*, cite the concrete measures (anonymization/aggregation, informed consent, algorithmic auditing, data-collection agreements, object-over-personal data) and the headline finding that **bottom-up, collaborative approaches with citizen/municipal data ownership** are the more ethical path.

# PART 4 — TWO ETHICAL CAMPS FOR BUSINESS, AND HOW TO DO AN ETHICAL ANALYSIS

## 4.1 Habermas discourse ethics vs. ethical strategists (Noland & Phillips, 2010)

The lecture frames a **quiz/debate**: *"In what camp of ethics do you believe as the best way to drive business — (A) Habermas discourse ethics, or (B) ethical strategists?"* *(Effing lecture, citing Noland & Phillips 2010)*. Both come from Noland & Phillips (2010) and both concern **how a firm should engage its stakeholders ethically** — but they differ on *why* and *how*.

**Camp A — Habermas discourse ethics** (covered in 2.3): stakeholder engagement is only moral if conducted through **pure, power-balanced, inclusive dialogue undertaken for its own sake**, following strict procedures, with all affected parties participating. Engagement done *as strategy* is amoral. It prefers **communicative reason** over instrumental reason. Strength: deeply democratic and fair. Weakness: costly, idealistic, risks power loss.

**Camp B — Ethical strategists** *(Effing lecture, Noland & Phillips 2010)*:

- Ethical engagement of stakeholders **not only may, but *ought to* be part of a firm's larger strategy.**
- Business **is** the creation of **value for all stakeholders** — for *all* groups and individuals who have a stake — and honest, open, fair engagement is **necessary for a business to function properly.**
- This is **not** the same as CSR or philanthropy; it still values something **other than (and over) pure shareholder wealth/profit.**
- Draws on the **Aristotle view** (live up to full potential) and rejects the **separation thesis** — i.e., **business value and stakeholder value cannot be cleanly separated.**
- Conceives **business as an essential part of the good life**; engaging stakeholders is a **necessary part of strategy** (a **utilitarian** justification — it produces the best overall result).
- The firm is **not an individual egoistic actor** but **represents a community of people** ("a firm with people and faces"); each employee is a **"social self"** embedded in webs of relationships (family, clubs, church, networks) that give them purposes **beyond** the firm.

**The key difference between the two camps:** Habermasians say engagement is moral **only if the *procedure* of dialogue is pure and non-instrumental** (engagement-as-strategy is amoral); ethical strategists say engagement **should be part of strategy** and is justified because **business is inherently about creating value for all stakeholders** (a more utilitarian, Aristotelian, integrated view). Put crudely: **Habermas = moral process for its own sake; ethical strategists = ethical engagement woven into strategy because it's good for business *and* right.**

**Exam angle:** Know that **both come from Noland & Phillips (2010)**, and be able to contrast them on the instrumental question: Habermas rejects instrumental/strategic engagement as amoral and demands pure communicative dialogue; ethical strategists embrace ethical engagement *as* strategy on the grounds that value-for-all-stakeholders is the purpose of business (rejecting the separation thesis). This is a prime "compare X and Y" candidate.

## 4.2 Ethical impact analysis — how to actually do it in the business case

This is the practical method the course wants you to apply *(Effing lecture)*. An **ethical impact analysis**:

- Examines **ethical implications**, which are **not necessarily a particular risk** (reinforcing the Day 2 distinction — an ethical issue ≠ a risk).
- Recognizes **various, potentially opposing views** (back to "no typical right answer").
- Requires you to **temporarily 'forget' your own opinions and views** — to reason from multiple stakeholders' perspectives rather than just your own.
- Directs you to **look at the UN Sustainable Development Goals and explicitly reflect on potential negative *and* positive external effects** of the IT innovation.

This is the method behind section 9 of the business-case outline (**Ethical analysis and CSR**), which asks for a *"reflection on the moral consequences … using various philosophical angles"* and an explanation of how the case *"maps to the United Nations sustainability and public responsibility goals"* *(Course manual, suggested outline)*. Note how this ties ethics directly to the **ECO/sustainability** learning goal (Day 4) — the UN SDGs are the shared hinge between the two topics.

**Exam angle:** If asked how to conduct an ethical analysis of an IT innovation, the method is: identify ethical implications (distinct from risks), consider multiple opposing stakeholder views, **set aside your own bias**, apply philosophical angles (Kant, Aristotle, Habermas, Levinas, Machiavelli), and **reflect on positive and negative external effects, mapping to the UN SDGs.** This is the recipe for the "write a reflection on moral consequences" learning goal.

# PART 5 — THE RISK vs. ETHICAL ISSUE DISTINCTION (carried over, near-certain on the exam)

This appeared in Day 2 and recurs here because it's in the norm-answer file verbatim and sits exactly on the ethics/risk boundary *(Norm-answer file; PMBOK; Effing lecture)*.

- A **risk** is an **uncertain event** that positively or negatively impacts the **process or outcomes of the project** (PMBOK). It affects primarily the **business outcome.**
- An **ethical issue** is a **potential impact with moral effects on stakeholders** in or outside the project. It can have **societal** impact, not just business impact. Whether it's perceived as good or bad **depends on the viewpoint and (moral) belief system of the stakeholder**; it can be viewed from a **philosophical angle**, differs in the extent of perceived good/bad (**morality**), and concerns **norms and values.**
- **A risk that is NOT an ethical issue:** a purely project-related matter such as a **limited budget overrun, unforeseen operational costs, or a temporary dip in performance** where no system depends on it for vital functionality — real project impact, but no moral/societal dimension.

**Exam angle:** This is almost certainly worth points. Reproduce the distinction (risk = uncertain event affecting project outcomes; ethical issue = viewpoint-dependent moral impact on stakeholders/society, about norms and values) and have the budget-overrun example ready as the "risk that is not an ethical issue."

# PART 6 — TYING IT TOGETHER (THE DAY 3 STORY IN ONE PASS)

Here's the connected narrative to hold for the exam:

**Ethics is not a formula for the right answer** — it's the disciplined study of what is good and right, accepting multiple legitimate perspectives and "no typical right answer," and it's distinct from **morality** (the norms we actually hold) *(Effing lecture)*. To reason about an IT innovation you apply **philosophical angles**: **Aristotle** (virtue, the good life, full potential), **Kant** (duty and the categorical imperative — universalizable rules, people as ends not means), **Habermas** (legitimacy through pure, inclusive, power-balanced dialogue; communicative over instrumental reason), **Levinas** (responsibility for the vulnerable Other), and **Machiavelli** (strategic, results-and-power realism). The marks come from showing how these **conflict** on a real case, not from declaring a winner.

The flagship application is the **smart city** — a contested, ICT-and-SIS-driven concept *(Caragliu et al. 2009; Ryan & Gregory 2019)* — where **Ryan & Gregory (2019)** identify four ethical issue categories: **conflicts of interest & bias** (vendors like IBM selling SIS while ranking cities; over-reliance on technical fixes over political/social solutions — Kitchin 2015), **economic pressure**, **inequalities** (job replacement, digital divide, rural decline), and **privacy** (tracking, recognition, security-vs-privacy). They're addressed through **anonymization, informed consent, algorithmic auditing, data agreements, object-over-personal data**, and above all **bottom-up, collaborative approaches with citizen/municipal data ownership.**

On *how a firm should be ethical*, **Noland & Phillips (2010)** offer two camps: **Habermas discourse ethics** (engagement is moral only as pure, non-instrumental dialogue) vs. **ethical strategists** (ethical stakeholder engagement *ought* to be woven into strategy, because business is the creation of value for *all* stakeholders — rejecting the separation thesis, drawing on Aristotle and a utilitarian justification). Finally, the practical method is an **ethical impact analysis**: surface implications (distinct from risks), weigh opposing views, set aside your own bias, apply the philosophical angles, and **reflect on positive and negative external effects mapped to the UN SDGs** — which is exactly the "reflection on moral consequences" the exam's heaviest learning goal demands, and the hinge into Day 4's sustainability topic.
