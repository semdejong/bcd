# CASE SCENARIO

**MedConnect** is a semi-government regional healthcare agency in the Netherlands responsible for coordinating patient data and care logistics across hospitals, clinics, and municipalities in its region. It is funded by public money and overseen by a regional health board, several municipal councils, and a national inspectorate.

MedConnect wants to launch an IT programme called **"CareGrid"** — a digital platform that uses AI and big data to (a) predict regional care demand, (b) automatically route patients to available capacity, and (c) give citizens an app to manage appointments and access their own health records. The programme bundles several interrelated projects (a data platform, a predictive-analytics engine, a citizen-facing app, and integration work with existing hospital systems). It is expected to run for about three years.

You have been hired as a **business-case consultant** to help MedConnect justify, structure, and prioritise the CareGrid investments. Several board members are nervous: a previous large IT project at the agency failed badly, running far over budget and launching with fewer features than promised.

*Answer all five questions with reference to the case where relevant.*

**B.** Name the five **cost categories** according to Ward & Daniel (2012), and for each give a CareGrid example. Briefly explain why the "cost of carrying out business changes" category is easy to underestimate. **(4 points)**

**B.** Explain the difference between **mitigation** and **contingency**, and state the two formulas that relate them to *reduction* and *exposure*. Apply this to one concrete CareGrid risk of your choice (give the risk, a mitigation, and a contingency). **(4 points)**

## Q1 — Benefits & Costs (10)

**A. Four benefit types + middle distinction + examples (6 pts)**

The four types, hardest-to-softest the other way (softest → hardest) *(Ward & Daniel 2012)*:
1. **Observable** — judged against criteria using **expert judgement**; you can tell it happened but only via informed opinion. *(0.5)*
2. **Measurable** — you **can measure** the current level **but cannot forecast the improvement** in advance. *(0.5)*
3. **Quantifiable** — the **improvement can be forecast** (you can put a number on the expected change). *(0.5)*
4. **Financial** — a **financial formula** can be applied to express the benefit in money. *(0.5)*

**The key middle distinction (2 pts):** a *measurable* benefit can be measured but the improvement **cannot be forecast**; a *quantifiable* benefit is one where the **improvement itself can be forecast**. (This is the classic trap — award full marks only if this distinction is explicit.)

**CareGrid examples (2 pts, 0.5 each):**
- Observable: "staff perceive routing decisions as fairer/clearer" (expert judgement).
- Measurable: current patient wait-times can be measured, but you can't yet forecast how much CareGrid will cut them.
- Quantifiable: "expected reduction in average appointment wait-time from 12 days to 7 days."
- Financial: "€X saved per year in administrative staff time from automated routing."

**B. Five cost categories + examples + the soft-cost point (4 pts)**

The five categories *(Ward & Daniel 2012)* — 0.5 each for naming + plausible example (2.5 total), plus 1.5 for the soft-cost explanation:
1. **Purchase costs** — hardware/software/services, e.g. cloud servers and the analytics-engine licence.
2. **Internal system development costs** (internal FTEs) — MedConnect's own developers integrating hospital systems.
3. **Infrastructure costs** — network/data-centre capacity for the platform.
4. **Cost of carrying out the business changes** (soft costs) — training clinical and admin staff on the new routing workflow.
5. **Ongoing costs** — annual maintenance, support, licence renewals.

**Why soft costs are underestimated (1.5 pts):** they're intangible and behavioural rather than a clear invoice — training, change management, and the productivity dip while people adapt are easy to leave out, yet (echoing the benefits-management philosophy) the technology only delivers value once people actually change how they work, so omitting them undervalues the true cost.

## Q2 — Risk Management (10)

**A. Risk definition + risk vs. uncertainty (3 pts)**

- **Risk (PMBOK):** an *uncertain event or condition that, if it occurs, has a positive or negative effect on project objectives.* (1 pt; bonus understanding if they note risk can be positive/an opportunity.)
- **Risk vs. uncertainty (Peixoto et al. 2014) (2 pts):** a **risk** has a **known probability** — the "**known unknowns**," susceptible to analysis; an **uncertainty** has an **unknown probability** — the "**unknown unknowns**," not susceptible to analysis. (1 pt for the known/unknown probability point, 1 pt for the known-unknowns/unknown-unknowns phrasing.)

**B. Mitigation vs. contingency + formulas + application (4 pts)**

- **Mitigation** reduces the **probability** of the risk; **contingency** reduces the **impact** if it occurs. (1.5 pts — full marks only if the probability-vs-impact split is correct.)
- Formulas (1.5 pts): **Reduction = Mitigation × Contingency**; **Exposure = Risk − Reduction**.
- Application (1 pt), e.g.: Risk = "patient data breach." *Mitigation* = encryption + access controls (lowers probability). *Contingency* = breach-response plan + offline backups (lowers impact).

**C. Four response strategies + best response for unsafe routing (3 pts)**

- Four strategies (2 pts): **Avoid, Mitigate, Transfer, Accept** (opportunity twins Explore/Enhance/Share also acceptable).
- Best response (1 pt): for a **high-impact safety risk**, **Avoid** (preventive) is most appropriate — e.g. don't let the engine auto-execute routing without human clinical sign-off; eliminate the unsafe pathway entirely. (Accept is wrong here; a mitigation answer with strong justification can earn the point.)

## Q3 — Ethics (12)

**A. Risk vs. ethical issue + example (3 pts)**

- A **risk** is an uncertain event affecting **project outcomes** (primarily the business outcome); an **ethical issue** is a potential **moral impact on stakeholders** in or outside the project, can have **societal** impact, is **viewpoint-dependent** (depends on a stakeholder's moral belief system), can be viewed from a **philosophical angle**, and concerns **norms and values**. (2 pts)
- CareGrid example that's an ethical issue, not just a risk (1 pt): e.g. *using predictive analytics on health data could entrench bias against certain neighbourhoods/groups* — a moral/societal concern about fairness, not merely a project-delivery risk. (A pure project risk like "budget overrun" would NOT earn this mark.)

**B. Two philosophical angles applied, reaching different conclusions (5 pts)**

2 pts per angle (1 for correct explanation, 1 for genuine application to CareGrid's data use), + 1 pt for showing they **diverge**. Example pairing:
- **Kant (deontology):** act only on universalizable principles; treat people as **ends, not means**. Applied: using citizens' health data without genuine consent treats them merely as a means to system efficiency and couldn't be universalized → **impermissible regardless of benefits.** (2)
- **Utilitarian/Machiavellian or ethical-strategist view:** judge by **outcomes** / aggregate welfare. Applied: if CareGrid's data use saves lives and cuts waiting times for the whole region, the aggregate good can justify it → **permissible.** (2)
- Divergence (1): Kant condemns the data use on principle even if outcomes are good; the consequentialist accepts it precisely *because* outcomes are good — the two reach opposite verdicts. (Any valid pairing that shows real tension earns the marks — e.g. Habermas demanding genuine citizen dialogue vs. Machiavelli accepting a top-down rollout.)

**C. Ryan & Gregory's four categories + two applied (4 pts)**

- Four categories (2 pts, 0.5 each): **Conflicts of interest & bias; Economic pressure; Inequalities; Privacy.**
- Two applied to CareGrid (2 pts, 1 each), e.g.:
  - *Privacy*: citizens' health records are highly sensitive — control/ownership of data, security-vs-privacy tension.
  - *Inequalities*: a citizen app assumes digital access — risk of a **digital divide** excluding elderly or low-income patients; or *bias*: over-reliance on a technical fix instead of addressing underlying care-capacity problems (Kitchin 2015).

## Q4 — Public Sector & Sustainability (10)

**A. Four public/private differences + why two make the case harder (6 pts)**

Four differences (2 pts, 0.5 each) from Winkler (2013) — any four of: **goals** (multifaceted public value vs. shareholder value); **stakeholders** (many/conflicting vs. few); **environment** (legal/formal constraints); **incentives** (low/soft budgets/scrutiny); **risks** (high aversion); **competition** (cooperation vs. competition); **IT innovation** (necessity vs. advantage); **IT competencies** (lower); **sourcing** (complex tendering); **resources** (shared).

Why two make the case harder (4 pts, 2 each), e.g.:
- **Multifaceted public value:** you can't justify CareGrid on NPV alone — you must argue value across equity, access, care quality, and accountability, which is harder to quantify and balance. (2)
- **Many conflicting stakeholders:** the health board, municipalities, the inspectorate, hospitals, and citizens have competing goals, so the business case must reconcile conflicting definitions of success rather than optimise one. (2) *(Complex tendering, risk-aversion, and scrutiny are equally valid choices.)*

**B. UN SDGs + mapping CareGrid (4 pts)**

- What the SDGs are (2 pts): **17** Sustainable Development Goals, **set in 2015**, **target 2030**, covering poverty, education, health, equality, climate, sustainable cities, etc.
- Mapping with positive + negative (2 pts): e.g. **positive** — CareGrid supports **Goal 3 (Good Health & Well-being)** by improving care access, and arguably **Goal 11 (Sustainable Cities & Communities)**; **negative/tension** — it may strain **Goal 10 (Reduced Inequalities)** via the digital divide if vulnerable citizens can't use the app. (1 pt for a sensible positive mapping, 1 pt for a sensible tension — reflecting on *both* directions is the key skill.)

## Q5 — Tools & Governance (8)

**A. IT portfolio + Strategic Grid + high-potential decision options (3 pts)**

- **IT portfolio** (1 pt): the **entirety of an organisation's IT investments** (all projects and programmes), managed to **select and prioritise** them for the best benefit of the organisation.
- **Strategic Grid** (1 pt) *(Ward & Peppard 2002, orig. McFarlan 1983)*: a 2×2 classifying investments as **Strategic** (critical to future strategy), **High Potential** (may be important to future success), **Key Operational** (currently depended on), **Support** (valuable but not critical). MedConnect can plot each CareGrid sub-project to decide where to invest/prioritise.
- High-potential decision options (1 pt): because high-potential investments are **uncertain/innovation-based**, the options are to **experiment / pilot / wait-and-see**, then **expand** if it proves out or **abandon** if it doesn't — i.e. exactly **real-options** thinking, rather than full commitment.

**B. Calculation — SmartTriage (5 pts)**

**Decision tree (1 pt for a correct structure):**
```
                          ┌─ 70% → €1,800k
        ┌─ Build (−€600k) ─┤
Decision┤                  └─ 30% → €700k
  node  │
        └─ Not build → €400k
```
*(Build = decision branch leading to a probability node with two outcomes; Not build = flat €400k.)*

**Expected value of building (2 pts):**
E[return | build] = (0.70 × €1,800k) + (0.30 × €700k) = €1,260k + €210k = **€1,470k**
Net of the €600k build cost: €1,470k − €600k = **€870k**

**Comparison + recommendation (2 pts):**
Building's net expected value (**€870k**) exceeds not building (**€400k**), so **MedConnect should build SmartTriage.** *(Award the marks for correct probability-weighting, subtracting the cost, and an explicit recommendation. Check: probabilities sum to 1 ✓.)*
